The Science of Bacteria!

What's really going on in that drop of seawater

← Back to the game

This game is a playful, simplified model of marine microbial ecology — the hidden world of bacteria, viruses, and single-celled predators that runs the ocean. Everything you do in it is based on real biology. Here's the science behind each piece, with links to read more. (Blue underlined words are Wikipedia links.)

01The ocean is mostly microbes

A single teaspoon of seawater holds around a million bacteria and ten million viruses. These bacterioplankton are the ocean's recyclers: they break down dead material and return its nutrients to the food web, a cycle scientists call the microbial loop. They're invisible, but they drive the planet's carbon and nutrient cycles.

In the game you play one bacterium in that world — a single cell trying to eat, grow, and survive.

02Marine snow: food falling from above

The big drifting particles you eat are marine snow — clumps of dead plankton, poop, and mucus that sink through the ocean carrying organic matter to the deep. Real particles come in every size, with tiny ones vastly outnumbering big ones — a power-law size distribution — which is exactly how the game spawns them.

Your particles (marine snow, fecal pellets, diatom shells, chitin) drift in, get eaten, and are recycled — with many small ones and a few big ones, just like the real sea.

03Eating with enzymes

A bacterium is too small to swallow a particle, so it digests food outside its body. It releases extracellular enzymes that chop big molecules into small ones through a reaction called hydrolysis, then absorbs the pieces. Each enzyme only works on one kind of molecule.

Pressing Space releases your loaded enzyme, dissolving the matching blocks into nutrients you soak up. Different foods need different enzymes — which you have to evolve.

04Three fuels: fat, protein, carbs

Living matter is built from three main macronutrients: lipids (fats), proteins, and carbohydrates (sugars). They don't carry the same energy: fat packs about 9 Calories per gram, while protein and carbs each carry about 4 — which is why fatty foods are so calorie-dense.

Your score is Calories eaten, weighted by composition: a lipid block is worth more than twice a carbohydrate block, so it pays to unlock lipase and hunt the fatty particles.

05Swimming: run and tumble

Many bacteria swim using a corkscrew-like flagellum. They can't steer precisely, so they use a trick called chemotaxis: swim straight ("run") while food is getting closer, and randomly reorient ("tumble") when it isn't. Over time this biased random walk carries them toward food.

Hold a direction to run; let go to tumble. The chemotaxis adaptation lets your autonomous cells sense and steer toward food on their own.

06Growing and dividing

Bacteria reproduce by binary fission — a cell grows, copies its DNA, and splits into two identical daughters. Because each cell becomes two, populations can grow exponentially: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16… A well-fed colony explodes in numbers astonishingly fast.

Eat enough and you elongate and divide. Your daughters forage on their own — you keep steering one, and the colony can go exponential. Each division is a new generation.

07Evolution on the fly

Bacteria don't only inherit genes from their parents — they can pick up brand-new genes from their surroundings, a process called horizontal gene transfer. One route is transduction, where a virus accidentally carries DNA from one cell to another. Cells can also crank up a trait by making extra copies of a gene (gene amplification), boosting its expression.

The rare gold phage gives your cell a random heritable adaptation. Your capabilities show as a genome strand, and expression appears as amplification (×2, ×3…) — and adaptations only spread when you divide.

08Viruses that hunt bacteria

The most common predators of bacteria are viruses called bacteriophages ("phages"). In the lytic cycle, a phage injects its DNA, hijacks the cell to build copies of itself, then bursts the cell open — releasing a "burst" of new phages to infect neighbours. Phages are the most abundant biological entities on Earth.

Green phages infect and lyse your cells, releasing more phages. An infected cell agitates, then bursts. They stick to particles and wait for cells to come feed.

09Kill the winner

Phages are picky: each one infects only bacteria closely related to the cell it came from. So whichever type of bacterium becomes most common is the one phages are best at finding and killing — an idea ecologists call "kill the winner." It stops any single type from taking over and keeps the community diverse, a constant evolutionary arms race between hosts and their viruses.

A virus is red if it can infect your current cell, green if it can't. As you evolve, which viruses are dangerous keeps changing — and a booming lineage draws its own plague.

10CRISPR: a bacterial immune system

Bacteria fight back against phages with CRISPR — a real adaptive immune system that stores snippets of past invaders' DNA and uses them to recognise and destroy those viruses on sight. The same molecular machinery is now the basis of modern gene editing.

With the CRISPR adaptation, viruses that can't infect you become food — you harvest the immune ones for energy.

11Chemical warfare

Microbes are chemists. Many produce antibiotics and other secondary metabolites — toxins that harm competitors or fend off predators. Bacteria really do make compounds that poison the protists trying to graze on them.

The antibiotic adaptation lets you release a toxin cloud that poisons nearby protists; leveling it up widens the cloud so it hits more of them at once.

12Getting eaten

Bacteria are prey too. Single-celled protists graze on them, an important link in the ocean food web. Predator and prey numbers rise and fall in linked cycles — more food means more predators, which crash the prey, which starves the predators — the classic predator–prey dynamics first described by Lotka and Volterra.

The big pink protists hunt your cells. Watch the chart: protist and bacteria numbers oscillate, each chasing the other, exactly like the real equations predict.

13Waiting out hard times

When conditions get bad, many microbes enter dormancy — they shut down and wait. Some form tough resting cysts or endospores that survive starvation, heat, and even centuries of time, then revive when things improve.

A starving cell forms a resistant cyst — dormant and hard to kill — until food returns and it can wake back up.

14Many ways to make a living

Because different genes suit different conditions, a species splits into varieties adapted to different niches — ecotypes, each filling its own ecological niche. This diversity is what makes a microbial community resilient: when one type is knocked back, another is ready to take its place.

The colored bands on the chart are your ecotypes and generations over time — you can literally watch evolution happen, lineage by lineage, as new colors rise and fall.

The real ocean is far messier and more wonderful than any game — but if playing this made a bacterium, a virus, or the microbial loop feel a little more alive, that's the point. Now go evolve something.
