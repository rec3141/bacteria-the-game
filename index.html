Bacteria!

forage · evolve · divide · endure

You are a single bacterium adrift in a drop of the sea. Dissolve the marine particles around you for food, harvest genes from rare viruses, and divide to make your evolution count — everything out here is trying to eat, infect, or outcompete you. Such is life.

How to play

Controls

  • ↑←↓→ / W A S D — run in a direction; let go to tumble (reorient randomly)
  • Space — release the loaded enzyme (dissolve the particle ahead) or antibiotic (poison nearby protists)
  • Tab — switch which enzyme/antibiotic is loaded  ·  M — toggle music
  • Shift — switch lineage: hand control to another of your lineages
  • Esc — pause & high scores  ·  Help button — this guide
  • On a phone: flick the left thumbstick toward a direction and the cell runs that way for ~3s on its own — no need to hold (tap the centre to stop); release / lineage sit along the bottom and ❚❚ pauses; tap a gene in the right-hand genome column to load that enzyme; swipe the chart (up top) to fold it away.

The loop

You can't swim through the big particles, but you can dissolve them with extracellular enzymes to reach the nutrients inside. Eat enough and you elongate and divide; daughters forage on their own and inherit everything you've evolved. Respiration burns energy every second. A cell that runs low on energy forms a resistant cyst until conditions improve.

Food — three resources

Particles are patchy mixes of lipid, protein and carbohydrate. Each needs its own enzyme, and you start able to digest only carbohydrate (carbohydrase).

Gold phages — adaptations 🧬

Chase down the rare gold phage (a star on the minimap, often buried in a particle) to gain a random heritable adaptation: a new enzyme (lipase/protease), higher enzyme expression, chemotaxis (auto-seek food), an antibiotic (an anti-protist toxin), or CRISPR (a phage defence).

Viruses — phages

Phages drift, stick to particles, and infect cells — but a virus only infects cells at a similar adaptation level to the host it came from, so it's colour-coded to you: red ones can infect your current cell, green ones can't. As you evolve, your level shifts, so which viruses read red or green changes over the run. An infected cell eventually lyses, releasing more phages.

The chart

The graph below the ocean stacks your bacteria by generation — each colour is a lineage at a given level of adaptation, a new shade each time a lineage adapts, so you can watch generations rise and fall (dormant cysts are counted in their generation's band). Lines overlay for protists and viruses, and a second graph tracks the food available. Your run's full story — and where each adaptation happened — is shown when you die.

The Science

This game is a playful, simplified model of marine microbial ecology — the hidden world of bacteria, viruses, and single-celled predators that runs the ocean. Everything you do is based on real biology. Here's the science behind each piece; the underlined words are Wikipedia links.

01The ocean is mostly microbes

A teaspoon of seawater holds around a million bacteria and ten million viruses. These bacterioplankton are the ocean's recyclers: they break down dead material and return its nutrients to the food web, a cycle called the microbial loop. They're invisible, but they drive the planet's carbon and nutrient cycles.

In the game you play one bacterium in that world — a single cell trying to eat, grow, and survive.

02Marine snow: food from above

The big drifting particles you eat are marine snow — clumps of dead plankton, poop, and mucus that sink through the ocean carrying organic matter to the deep. Real particles come in every size, with tiny ones vastly outnumbering big ones — a power-law size distribution — exactly how the game spawns them.

Particles (marine snow, fecal pellets, diatom shells, chitin) drift in, get eaten, and recycle — many small, a few big, like the real sea.

03Eating with enzymes

A bacterium is too small to swallow a particle, so it digests food outside its body: it releases extracellular enzymes that chop big molecules into small ones through hydrolysis, then absorbs the pieces. Each enzyme works on only one kind of molecule.

Space releases your loaded enzyme, dissolving the matching blocks into nutrients. Different foods need different enzymes — which you evolve.

04Three fuels: fat, protein, carbs

Living matter is built from three macronutrients: lipids (fats), proteins, and carbohydrates (sugars). They don't carry the same energy: fat packs about 9 Calories per gram, protein and carbs about 4 — which is why fatty food is so calorie-dense.

Your score is Calories eaten, weighted by composition: a lipid block is worth more than twice a carb one — so it pays to unlock lipase and hunt fatty particles.

05Swimming: run and tumble

Many bacteria swim with a corkscrew flagellum. They can't steer precisely, so they use chemotaxis: swim straight ("run") while food gets closer, reorient randomly ("tumble") when it doesn't. Over time this biased random walk carries them toward food.

Hold a direction to run; let go to tumble. The chemotaxis adaptation lets your autonomous cells sense and steer toward food.

06Growing and dividing

Bacteria reproduce by binary fission — a cell grows, copies its DNA, and splits in two. Because each cell becomes two, populations grow exponentially: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16… a well-fed colony explodes astonishingly fast.

Eat enough and you elongate and divide. Daughters forage on their own — you keep steering one, and the colony can go exponential. Each division is a new generation.

07Evolution on the fly

Bacteria don't only inherit genes from parents — they pick up brand-new genes from their surroundings, called horizontal gene transfer. One route is transduction, where a virus carries DNA between cells. Cells can also crank up a trait by making extra copies of a gene (amplification), boosting its expression.

The rare gold phage gives a random heritable adaptation. Your genome strand shows your genes, expression appears as amplification (×2, ×3…), and adaptations only spread when you divide.

08Viruses that hunt bacteria

The most common predators of bacteria are viruses called bacteriophages. In the lytic cycle, a phage injects its DNA, hijacks the cell to build copies of itself, then bursts it open — releasing a "burst" of new phages. Phages are the most abundant biological entities on Earth.

Red phages infect and lyse your cells, releasing more phages. They stick to particles and wait for cells to feed.

09Kill the winner

Phages are picky — each infects only bacteria closely related to the cell it came from. So whichever type becomes most common is the one phages are best at finding and killing — ecologists call this "kill the winner." It stops any one type from taking over and keeps the community diverse, a constant arms race between hosts and their viruses.

A virus is red if it can infect your current cell, green if it can't. As you evolve, which viruses are dangerous changes — and a booming lineage draws its own plague.

10CRISPR: bacterial immunity

Bacteria fight back with CRISPR — a real adaptive immune system that stores snippets of past invaders' DNA and uses them to recognise and destroy those viruses. The same machinery is now the basis of modern gene editing.

With CRISPR, viruses that can't infect you become food — you harvest the immune ones for energy.

11Chemical warfare

Microbes are chemists. Many make antibiotics and other secondary metabolites — toxins that harm competitors or fend off predators. Bacteria really do make compounds that poison the protists grazing on them.

The antibiotic adaptation releases a toxin cloud that poisons nearby protists; leveling it up widens the cloud to hit more at once.

12Getting eaten

Bacteria are prey too. Single-celled protists graze on them, a key link in the ocean food web. Predator and prey numbers rise and fall in linked cycles — more prey feeds more predators, which crash the prey, which starves the predators — the classic predator–prey dynamics.

The big pink protists hunt your cells. On the chart, protist and bacteria numbers oscillate, each chasing the other, just as the equations predict.

13Waiting out hard times

When conditions get bad, many microbes enter dormancy and wait. Some form tough resting cysts or endospores that survive starvation, heat, even centuries, then revive when things improve.

A starving cell forms a resistant cyst — dormant and hard to kill — until food returns and it can wake up.

14Many ways to make a living

Because different genes suit different conditions, a species splits into varieties adapted to different niches — ecotypes, each filling its own niche. This diversity makes a community resilient: knock one type back and another takes its place.

The coloured bands on the chart are your ecotypes and generations over time — you watch evolution happen, lineage by lineage, as colours rise and fall.

15The music is real DNA

The ambient soundtrack isn't composed — it's sonified from an actual gene. Each letter of a real 16S ribosomal RNA sequence from E. coli — the very gene biologists use to identify and classify bacteria — is read base by base (A, C, G, T) and mapped to notes in a minor scale, so you are literally listening to a bacterium's DNA.

Press M to toggle it. The melody you hear is one pass through the 16S gene; the low drone and echo are just for mood.

The real ocean is far messier and more wonderful than any game — but if this made a bacterium, a virus, or the microbial loop feel a little more alive, that's the point. Now go evolve something.
